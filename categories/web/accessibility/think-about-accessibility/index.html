Think about accessibility

Aug 12, 2018 • Austin Breslin

#accessibility #web

Why we all need to start working on accessibility now and not later? Well that is because we all want to deliver an excellent product for our users, we want to display our skills and be proud of the thing that we built. We want everyone to share in this experience.

How annoyed are you when users give you negative feedback and don’t lie about this. I know that feedback means that you can make your product even better and who doesn’t want to make a better product to learn and grow.

Well imagine that there are many people out there who can’t use your product as it stands right now, they won’t give you feedback because they are used to poor experiences no matter where they go?

Or since your a developer, I’ll assume that you are a power user with your keyboard, imagine if your IDE didn’t have your keyboard shortcuts, think I’m wrong? Try to stop using copy and paste.

If you haven’t built a website that is accessible, you have inadvertently built a site that is missing your own favorite features, now doesn’t this sound silly? This is why you need to start doing accessibility now.

FYI: I am not a UX/UI Developer

Accessibility is usually completely mis-represented within the web development community. When you think of accessibility you probably go straight into thinking about ARIA, as if it just something that gets tacked onto your website and this will solve all your problems.

After having worked in the computer industry for so long professionally, since IE 8, I see businesses think that ARIA is the solution to an accessible web.

So what does ARIA achieve?

ARIA is a tool to fill in the gaps between Visual and Non-Visual users. It will not solve all accessibility problems for you because it isn’t context aware, it’s not AI after all. We the web dev are the only people/things that are context aware in the process of designing the user experience (UX).

What is an accessible web?

My definition would be:

  • An accessible website is a website that caters for all types of users and provides them all a comparable experience.

How you achieve this is by thinking about all your users types at all times during the SDLC. Are there any tips for this? A simple technique that I have heard from many UX developers is that you should give each of your user types a name and characteristics. This is an analog hack to make it easier for you and your whole team.

Conclusion:

This post is getting too long, so I will follow up with a post about the personas that every website needs to use.

It’s hard to convince people to change even if they know they should. This post was an attempt to for all of us to identify the missing features, yes features within our website.

Thinking about accessibility is about thinking about the usability of your website for all of your users including yourself.
