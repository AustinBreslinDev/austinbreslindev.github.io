    Background:

    Nearly everyone who is a developer or IT technician has an interesting story about how they got introduced to programming.

    There was a game that came with the computer that was in my house that I played a lot, maybe too much. The computer was becoming slow. I decided that I would fix this.

    I took the computer apart, not really knowing if that would solve the problem but at least it would give me a clue. My family watched in horror, because they thought I had broken the computer. I could not imagine me watching a child take apart a computer without jumping in to stop them.

    I put the computer back together and somehow stumbled accross disk cleanup and disk defrag and this resolved the problem. I could now play the game again without losing any FPS.

    The progression:

    Not satisfied with the results though, I decided to make more adjustments to the computer. I found GNU/Linux.

    After installing Linux, I found that the screen resoultion was wrong, the graphics card was not supported. I spent around 4 weeks reading forums trying to find a solutino. Most of the time was wasted playing around with the options to be honest.

    This is when I decided to make Linux my OS of choice, even the hard stuff was understandable and people wanted to help. A big difference from the OS I was using before.

    Introduction to Programming:

    There was a person in the local community center who was advertising teaching children how to write HTML. I decided to go and see, as I thought it would be an intorduction to programming.

    Although it was good, it was not what I was looking for. Stumped as to where to go next in computers, I stopped. There was about a 4 to 5 year pause in my deveolopment in computers. When I went to college, doing nanotechnology I was reintroduced into programming through a scrience programming language, that is copyright protected so I won't name it.

    The copyrighted language was a good, but a carrer in nanotechnology wasn't a carrer in programming. After the first year of nanotechnology, I decided to leave and go do a programming degree instead.

    The road to becoming a developer:

    After leaving the programming degree there was no real progression for me, in my opinion. I was applying for anything releated to computers at the time. After about 9 months, I found an internship with a local company.

    This is where I meet a developer who was willing to mentor me and answer any and all questions that I had to ask. Some of these questions were insane now that I look back at them. I once asked which was faster JSF or JSP and for him to explain the difference. I even asked him if client side or server side was the way to go.

    His response to these questions was amazing, he even proposed that I not look at what technique was faster but at what was best for the problem at hand. He even made me question the programming language that I knew and the company used.

    Through these questions and through his guidence I built a full stack system many times over. One JSP, One JSF, one using JavaScript and Servlets, one using Typescript and Servlets. All so I could see what he was trying to get me to understand.
